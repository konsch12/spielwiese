Session 1

What is Demography?

Micro and Macro Level

Preston et al. p.2: “Demography is one of the social science disciplines where micro- and macro-level analyses find perhaps their most complete and satisfactory articulation.”

Population structure by age and sex

Multidimensional: education, area of residence and ethnicity

Components of Population change

Endogenous Mechanisms:

  • Birth

  • Death

Exogenous Mechanisms:

  • In-migration

  • Out-migration

Population stock = size and composition of a given population at given point in time

Population flows:

Events Processes
Births Fertility
Deaths Mortality
Marriages Nuptiality
In/out migration Migration

Balancing Equation of Population Change

Balancing Equation

\[ N(T) = N(0) = B[0, T] - D[0, T] + I[0, T] - O[0, T] \]

\(N(T)\) = #person alive in population at time \(T\)

\(N(0)\) = #person alive in population at time \(0\)

\(B[0, T]\) = #births in pop. between time \(0\) and \(T\)

\(D[0, T]\) = #deaths in pop. between time \(0\) and \(T\)

\(I[0, T]\) = #in-migrations between time \(0\) and \(T\)

\(O[0, T]\) = #out-migrations between time \(0\) and \(T\)

Definitions

Natural increase/decrease = \(B[0, T] - D[0, T]\)

Net migration = \(I[0, T] - O[0, T]\)

  • Comprehensive

  • Exact

  • Used for indirect estimation of net-migration

Measuring population changes

  • Count number of occurrences

    • impractical

    • heavily influenced by #people who are at risk of experiencing the event (e.g: increasing population size)

  • Vital events

    • births, death, marriages, in/out-migration

  • Demographic Data

    • parish records, civil registration, census, sample surveys

  • Combining flows to population stocks

    • e.g: #deaths per 100 people

Person time at risk

  • not all members equally exposed

  • frequency of occurrence higher the longer the exposure

  • PYL = sum of each individuals time at risk of experiencing an event

Calculating PYL

\[ PYL[0, T] = \sum_{t = 1}^4{N_t\Delta t} \]

Continuous time:

\[ PYL[0, T] = \int_{t = 0}^T{N_t dt} \]

Demographic Rate

= demographic rate is an indicator that properly relates the frequency of an event to the size of population exposed to the event

\[ Rate = \frac{\# \text{occurrences [0, T]}}{\text{Person-time of exposure [0, T]}} \]

Crude Death Rate:

\[ CDR[0, T] = \frac{\# \text{deaths [0, T]}}{\text{Person-time of exposure [0, T]}} \]

Crude Birth Rate: \[ CBR[0, T] = \frac{\# \text{births [0, T]}}{\text{Person-time of exposure [0, T]}} \]

Crude In-Migration Rate: \[ CRIM[0, T] = \frac{\# \text{in-migrations [0, T]}}{\text{Person-time of exposure [0, T]}} \]

Ratio, Proportion and Rate

Approximation of PYL

PYL Approximation

Midyear method:

\[ PYL = N_{mid} * T \]

Average population method:

\[ PYL(0, T) = \frac{N(T) + N(0)}{2} * T \]

Session 2

Population Growth Rates

Instantaneous Growth Rate

Definition

Instantaneous growth rate = crude growth rate at a point in time or in very tiny interval of time

Important

\[ r(t) = \frac{dN(t)}{dt} * \frac{1}{N(t)} = \frac{dln(N(t))}{dt} \] This represents the fraction by which a population would grow during a tiny period of time.

Exponential Growth Model

Exponential growth model

\[N(t) = N(0)e^{\int_0^T{r(t)dt}}\]

For constant \(r(t)\) between 0 and T at \(r^{*}\): \[N(t) = N(0)e^{Tr^{*}}\]

Constant growth assumption

Assumption: \(r(t) = r^*\)

\[ r^* = ln(\frac{N(T)}{N(0)}) * \frac{1}{T}\]

In a closed population -> constant change in RNI

\(r^* = ln(1 + \frac{B(t)-D(t)}{N(0)}) * \frac{1}{T}\)

Doubling Time

The time it would take for a population to double in size at a given growth rate, assuming the exponential model holds.

Doubling time

\[ T_{double} = ln(2) * \frac{1}{r^*} \]

Estimating Period PYL

Estimating PYL under constant exponential growth rate assumption.

PYL (constant growth)

\[ PYL(0,T) = \frac{N(T) - N(0)}{r^*} \]

Limitations of midyear approach

  • If N(t) is linear -> good approximation => use for short time intervals

  • Under constant exponential growth rate assumption -> underestimation

Session 3

Problems of Crude Rates

  • Violate principle of correspondence
    • loose connection between risk of occurrence and exposure
  • Too “crude” to compare demographic experiences among populations
    • does not take population structure into account
    • => differences in demographic composition greatly influence principal crude rates

Age-Specific Rates

Age-specific death rate

Single ages:

\[ ASDR_x[0, T] = \frac{\# \text{deaths of people age x years [0, T]}}{PYL_x[0, T]} \]

\[ M_x = \frac{D_x}{PYL_x} \]

Age groups of size \(n\), \([x, x + n)\):

\[ nM_x[0, T] = \frac{nD_x}{nPYL_x} \]

Crude Death Rate

CDR is the sum of ASDR weighted by the proportionate age distribution of the population.

CDR expressed as sum of ASDRs

\[ CDR = \sum{ASDR_i*c_i} \]

where \(\sum{c_i} = 1\)

Minimizing the confounding effect of age composition

Age Specific Rates

  • ensures comparability among populations from within a specific age-group (despite different age-structures)

  • hard to summarize or interpret

Age Standardization

  • Asses impact of ASDR based on age structure of a standard population

  • ASDR of a population is weighted by proportionate age distribution of a standard population

ASCDR

\[ ASCDR^j = \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty}{M^j_iC^s_i} \]

\(M_s^j\) = age-specific death rates in population j

\(C_i^s\) = age-specific population weights of the standard population

Which population as standard?

  • One of the comparing populations

    • not recommended, different results for different standards

  • Average of the two population compositions

    • \(C_i^s = \frac{C^B_i + C^A_i}{2}\)

    • When comparing more than 2 populations:

      • Use standard close to mean or median of population structures

      • Use regional age-structure

Indirect Standardization

Standardized Comparative Mortality Ratio

Summary measure to compare the death rates in two populations whilst accounting for different population distributions

  • data on ASDR unavailable/fluctuating
  • small populations
Definition

SMR = the ratio of the actual number of deaths in population \(A\) to the expected number based on the age-specific death rates of the standard population

SMR

\[ SMR = \frac{\sum_i{D_i^A}}{\sum_i{M^S_iN_i^A}} \]

If \(SMR < 1\), \(M_iS > M^A_i\).

Period vs. Cohort Approach

Period Approach

Advantages:

  • readily available data

  • easy to interpret

Disadvantages:

  • violate the principle of correspondence

  • does not reflect experience of real group of people

Cohort Approach

  • real group of people who experience demographic events during a specific time interval, e.g: marriage cohort, school cohort, …

  • Birth cohort = real group of people sharing the same birth date

Cohort rate: \(cohort rate = [x, x+n] = \frac{\# \text{events in the cohort [x, x+n]}}{\text{PYL in the cohirt [x, x+n]}}\)

Probability

= chance that some event will occur, not rate at which it occurs

\(Probability = \frac{\#Occurrences}{\# \text{preceeding events or trials}}\)

\(nqx^c = \frac{\#\text{Occurrences in cohort c [x, x+n]}}{\# \text{persons in the cohort reaching age. x}}\)

The Identification Problem

Measures base on the cohort and period approaches are interlinked: Period = Age + Cohort

How do we measure age?

  • Exact age

    • exact number in years, months and days

    • rarely used

  • Age reached during the year

    • remains constant during the whole calendar year

  • Age reached last birth day/age in complete years

    • remains constant until next birthday

=> disparity between age reached during year and age last birthday

Lexis Diagram

Life Lines

  • each person in LD has a life line

  • diagonal (45°) line

  • starts at birth, ends at death

Ways of Classifying Events

Age-Period Classification

  • Classification according to age and calendar time

  • Age in completed years

  • Corresponds to two different cohorts

  • Rectangle

  • A - exposure/events by those who reached age 3 in the previous year

  • B - reach age 3 in the calendar year

Age-Cohort Classification

  • grouped together by age in completed years and within the same birth cohort

  • parallelogram, two horizontal lines (ages), two 45° lines (cohort)

  • counting events will stretch over two calendar years

  • Period = Cohort + age

Period-Cohort Classification

  • events/exposure occurred in a given calendar year and within a given cohort

  • parallelogram, two vertical lines (period), two 45° lines (cohort)

  • Age reached = period - cohort

  • Cohort will reach age x on average halfway through the period

Summary:

Person-time at risk in the Lexis Diagram

\(C\) …. cohort

\(x\) …. age

\(t\) … period

A-C: \(PYL^{C}_{[1, 2)} = \frac{P^C_x + P^C_{x+1}}{2} = P^{t+1}_{[x, x+ 1]}\)

Fertility and Reproduction

Period TFR = average number of children who would be born to a hypothetical cohort of women

Assumptions:

  • if they had all survived to the end of their reproductive period

  • bear children at each age at the rate observed during a particular period

Reproductive measures

  • TFR > 2.1 -> population is growing

  • TFR < 2.1 -> population

TFR as growth indicator ignores two factors: sex ratio and mortality

Comparing the sizes of successive generations (comparing #daughters and #mothers) -> ASFR using female births

\[ nF_x^f = \frac{\# \text{female births to women age }[x, x + n]}{\text{PYL of women age }[x, x + n]} \]

Gross Reproduction Rate

GRR = number of daughters an average women would have if she lived through the end of their reproductive span

Net Reproduction Rate

= total number of daughter each member of a birth cohort of women produce during their reproduction life span if they were subject to the observed ASFR and ASMR

-> NRR is simply GRR adjusted or mortality

GRR and NRR:

\(l_0\) = original size of the women birth cohort

\(nL_x^f\) = PYL by members of the cohort of women in age interval \([x, x+n]\)

\(nF_x^f * nL_x^f\) = number of daughters born to between \([x,x+n]\) to a birth cohort of women

Average number of daughters born to a cohort of size \(l_0\)

\[ NRR = \frac{\sum{F_i^F * nL_x^f}}{l_0}\] ????

  • If NRR > 1, cohort of girls will replace a bigger cohort of daughters than themselves
  • If NRR = 1, the next generation will be exactly the same size as the present one
  • If NRR < 1, implies that each generation is smaller than the previous generation, so the population will decline from natural decline across the generations
Note

The stable population assumption is crucial when interpreting the NRR, meaning it is assumed that the structure of a population does not change over time.

  • Constant fertility and mortality rates

Calculating PRR

\(W\) … original cohort size

\(W_j\) … #women at parity \(i\)

\(W = \sum_{j = 0}^{I}{W_j}\)

\(P_j\) … #women at parity \(j\) or more

\(P_j = \sum_{a= j}^{I}{W_a}\)

\[ PRR_{j, j+1} = \frac{\text{Women at Parity j + 1 or more }}{\text{Women at Parity j or more}} = \frac{P_{j+1}}{P_j}\]

Completed cohort fertility

\[ CCF = \sum_{i = 1}{PPR(0,i)} \]

Order Specific TFR

Birth order refers to the rank of a birth according to the number of previous births to the mother.

\[ F_{i, j, t} = \frac{\text{#births of order j to women in age group i}}{} \]
